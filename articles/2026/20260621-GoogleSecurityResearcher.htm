Google security researcher denied earnings

21st June 2026

Following on from Microsoft's recent spat, Google decided it wanted in on the action in denying researchers the finders fee they promised. In a recent bug report marked as high priority with a representative telling bug hunter, Justin O'Leary, "Nice Catch!", Google made a U-turn and decided that it wasn't an issue after all, but by design. Ah yes, that old chestnut eh?

Justin has published a nice blog article about the vulnerability which allows complete organisation takeover in Kubernetes namespaces with zero GCP permission.

After the initial praise in finding the bug, Google responded by saying "Cloud Vulnerability Reward Program panel has decided that the security impact of this issue does not meet the criteria to qualify for a reward.". Why so? you may ask? - "Rationale: We have reviewed your report and determined that the issue you described is working as intended. The Config Connector Service Account requires the organization admin role for the reported scenario to be exploitable which is not recommended and breaks best practice.".

There is but one small fly in the soup, however - and that being that Google actually demonstrates this practice in their help documents. Oooops!

So, how do we think security researchers with tasty zero-days are feeling right now? Pretty underappreciated and ready to drop hot potatoes all over the Internet is probably how you would expect. What the fallout of these big name brands reneging on their security research bounties will be over the coming months and years will be interesting to watch closely. We could either see responsible investigations and support hardening platforms dwindling, leading to epic unknown vulnerabilities, or a reduction in responsible disclosure and a higher quantity of public vulnerability information being thrown across the Internet.

One can only imagine the heated conversations between staff right now on both sides of this argument.
