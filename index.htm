Discord Information leaked

Well, well, well, if it isn't the consequences of enforced age verification decisions coming back to bite you... brace for more of this over the coming weeks, months and years.

Discord has said around 70,000 users have had their data potentially leaked by a company that was contracted to undertake age verification. The information leaked includes photographs for age verification, personal details, support team chat information and partial credit card details. So just a few things to be concerned about.

Affected users have been contacted by Discord and the (surprise, surprise) third-party that was connected to Discord's systems has had access removed.

Again we see the side-chain attack in full flow with outsourced support companies failing in their duty to protect information. The list of companies affected by this type of attack is growing daily.

With the requirement for age verification firmly in place on many services, there is a need for the service providers to step-up their security. The rush to implement providers will undoubtedly have led to cost saving at the detriment to security.

Let's see what the actual toll is on Discord and which provider will be the next leaky bucket.

Metropolitan Police arrest suspected nursery group hackers

The Metropolitan Police have confirmed they have arrested two 17-year-old individuals in the ongoing investigation into the Kido chain nursery hack that saw the names, addresses and photographs of children and their parents stolen in late September.

The hackers initially demanded a ransom of around £600,000 in Bitcoin to not release the details and began a campaign of contacting parents in the list to have them pressurise the nursery chain. When ransom money was seen to be not forthcoming the hackers began to publish the images and addresses of children in their data.

If not already calous enough in setting a nursery as a target, this act of seems to have tipped the scales heavily against the criminals with the group suddenly blurring imagery on their site seemingly worried about negative reputation with other hacker groups.

On the 2nd October the hackers suddenly removed all the stolen data and pictures from their site, claiming to have deleted all 8,000 files stating "No more remains and this can comfort parents".

Was it external pressure from other groups, concern over the backlash in the media or some flake of moral concern? As yet we'll have to speculate.

The Metropolitan Police confirmed the hackers were arrested at residential addresses in Bishop's Stortford, Hertfordshire, on suspicion of computer misuse and blackmail. The investigation continues.

OpenAI boots suspected Chinese Gov accounts from ChatGPT

OpenAI published an intelligence report stating it has banned ChatGPT accounts it believes to be linked to Chinese government entities attempting to use AI models to generate surveillance software.

The users, now banned, were suspected to be utilising a VPN service to access the AI software from China. In one account, their requests to the AI models were seen to be asking it to design promotional materials and project plans for a social media listening tool, described as a "probe" that could scan X, Facebook, Instagram, Reddit, TikTok and YouTube for what the user described as extermist speech, ethnic, religious and political content.

The principal investigator on OpenAI's intelligence and investigations team said "What we saw and banned in those cases was typically threat actors asking ChatGPT to help put together plans or documentation for AI-powered tools, but not then to implement them.".

The company also banned one user who asked ChatGPT to identify funding sources for an X account that criticised the Chinese government, and another who asked ChatGPT to identify organizers of a petition in Mongolia

Michael Flossman, who leads OpenAI's threat intelligence team said "One China-linked cluster that we investigated, for example, used ChatGPT to draft phishing lures and then explored another model, DeepSeek, to automate mass targeting,".

China isn't alone in being banned as OpenAI has also seen system abuse originating from Russia asking the model to develop and refine malware, including remote access tojans, credential stealers and features to help malware evade detection.

AI is a growing concern for data exfiltration and as a weaponised tool allowing those who would normally be incapable of generating malware and campaigns the means to quickly and easily generate systems. We can only hope that the teams continue to implement stronger guard-rails and keep on top of security issues as we get to grips with the automated future we've released on ourselves.

Malicious Python Package

A malicious package on the Python Package Index (PyPI) repository has been caught and removed, but downloaded 2653 times since it appeared on the 26th September. Claiming to offer a SOCKS5 proxy service, it included malicious functionality to provide stealthy backdoors to Windows to enable the drop of additional payloads onto the machine.

JFrog's security research team provided a full breakdown of the package citing:

"The SoopSocks PyPI package (XRAY-725599) promises to create a SOCKS5 proxy service and report information about the server to a configurable Discord webhook, supporting fallback as a scheduled task. While providing this capability it exhibits behaviour as a Backdoor proxy server, targeting Windows platforms, using automated installation processes via VBScript or an executable version."

The malicious package included privilege escalation and reconnaissance capabilities, making it a danger to developer systems and servers.

The team reported the package to maintainers after their analysis, and it was subsequently removed from PyPI on Sep 29, 18:00 GMT+3.

Chrome High Severity Patch

Chrome has had a high severity patch pushed by Google which is under active exploitation. If you haven't already, now is the time to run updates in your browser.

The flaw in the V8 JavaScript and WebAssembly engine, tracked as CVE-2025-10585, is a type confusion bug where the engine misinterprets a block of memory as one type of object when it's actually something else. This flaw can lead to system crashes, arbitrary code execution, and when chained with other outstanding issues, potential full system compromise via a malicious HTML page.

The flaw was discovered and reported by the Google Threat Analysis Group and a patch for Windows, Apple and Linux environments is available now.

Hackers turns 30

It can't be - I refuse to believe that so many years have passed under the network bridge... Okay I caught sight of my face in the mirror, yes I can. Hackers was released 30 years ago today in the 'States. It was a month later on the 13th October before those of us on this side of the pond could bask in the glorious cybercopia of this cult classic.

It's a film that so many of us have gathered around over the years, a pop culture tongue-in-cheek snapshot of the hacking subculture in the early 1990's. Making it's way into our shared lexicon with references littered around in our daily lives, I think it stands as a dividing line of ethos in the industry. It was a very different world back then, with exploration mainly for the fun and knowledge increase. The big shift to financial exploitation happened post-Hackers. Yes, I know, you're going to argue AT&T's timecode change was for financial gain, but it wasn't at the indiscriminant detriment to the people at large that we see today in ransomware.

Thirty years of change in the industry has brought new technology, methodologies, landscapes and cultures into play and we of the older generations remember a time when things were simpler, more open and maliable as protocols and systems came into being. We remember the days of pagers and dialups, with time online limited.

Hackers remains in my top ten list of favourite films all these years down the line, despite it's hilarious and obvious failings, despite the avant-garde nature of it compared to the people the teams followed in real life, despite the wild shoe-horning of terminology. It's the perfect imperfect film about a cultural group that was adventuring in the early digital realm. A snapshot of psyche from a different age. In a year that also saw The Net released (yes, that's 30 years old this year also - no there's no secret button on my site... or is there...) and Johnny Mnemonic (yes, that's also 30 years old, geez, what, did you give up your childhood memories, oh wait...) we felt as if we were on the crest of something magical.
Then the mega corporations moved in to our neighbourhood...

There is much we've gained since Hackers was released, and much we have lost along the way. In a world where ZeroCool probably now works as CSO at the oil company Plague did; where Cereal Killer runs an Internet radio station (oh, wait..) and Burn is CISO for Apple, we all grew up watching the digital world form and evolve around us as we longed for the thrill of the adventure on hardware that is less powerful now than our washing machines and fridges.

The birthday of Hackers reminds us that the ideals and goals we shaped the digital world with are still there, just under the dusty layers of piling storage mediums. Find the disk, drop it in the drive and boot up the memories of what got you into all this computing business. There's a world to explore and discover - it's choice man, pure choice. This was our world, the world of the electron and the switch, the beauty of the baud. -[Hacker Manifesto]

I miss the days of finding amazing items at computer faires for crazy cheap prices; I miss full tower PC's with SCSI cards and CRT's, 5.75 inch and 3.5 inch disks. I miss Zip disks and Jazz drives, chunky storage for the masses you didn't lose in your bag. I miss the innovation and the eccentric devices available on the market before the time of homogeneous laptops and PC's. Before the corporate shareholders determined profits were more important than innovation and individuality. Perhaps that's why there's such a burgeoning cyberdeck community?

We need to remember, in the face of a darker and more hate-filled digital domain, Hackers of the world - unite. We demanded free access to information, well that came at a price but they revoked on the deal.

So this year, on this 30th anniversary of Hackers, I'll be watching it again with a laptop and notebook. I'll be shouting "Hack the Planet!" appropriately all day and remembering fondly a time when BBS boards and Telnet chat sessions were our communications channels. When we dreamed of having too much machine in our hands as we sat in a hacker club with electronic music blaring, coffee flowing and hardware being sold outside.

So Nintendo generation, remember to trust your technolust and rediscover the mindset of adventure, learning and excitement that the years of corporate coalface tore from our memory banks.

Happy birthday, Hackers. Here's to the next 30 years together... we're elite.

NPM Packages Compromised

Due to a targeted and fortuitous phishing attack on a developer, twenty popular NPM packages were compromised and infected with malware. Prompting the co-maintainer to enter MFA information, the attackers gained access to the NPM package maintainer's account.

The malware and activity was thankfully caught fairly quickly by the teams and those affected by the incident are relatively limited. The malware-laiden NPM packages have been removed from the system and an analysis of downloads by teams shows a limited issue.

NPM packages have a habit of being part of a chain of packages to build larger software, and this type of attack with packages used by millions of users highlights the effectiveness of sidechain attacks. Thankfully, in this instance, these compromised packages were only available between 13:30 and 15:00 UTC on the 8th September before the community cleaned-up the mess in the NPM packages. If your system has pulled packages in this period you are guaranteed to have been infected and are urged to update packages imediately.

The issue with any chained dependancies is that your supplier or their package might have been compromised before you included other software into your development proecess. Generating a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) is a good idea to understand your software and it's dependant items. Utilising an SBOM list, you can run a simple grep for the affected packages to ensure you're not including them within your chain.

More information is available on the Socket.Dev blog. As always, stay safe out there developing in the World Wild Web.

Fake NPM Package Injects Code

An NPM package impersonating a legitimate email library has been found by researchers. The package has thankfully now been removed from NPM with a warning.

The package, nodejs-smtp, had an identical tagling, page style and README to the legitimate nodemailer package and has been available since April 2025 garnering 347 downloads since it was added to the npm registry.

The package uses Electron tooling to unpack Atomic Wallet's app.asar, replacing the vendor bundle with a malicious payload before repackaging the application and removing process traces by deleting the working directory. The primary objective of this is to overwrite the recipient address with hard-coded wallets redirecting Bitcoin, Ethereum, Tether, XRP and Solana transactions.

Still operating as a mailer, it hides the underlying functionality to swipe cryptocurrency in normal operation as an SMTP maailer.

It's not the first time we've seen issues with downstream pacakages in this type of attack and it won't be the last.

South Korean Telecom Fined for terrible security

One for the scrapbook of "what in the hell, my good man?", SK Telecom have mismanaged their network systems security so utterly terribly that the watchdog has fined them $97M for their sloppy practices. The breach, disclosed back in April, led to the unauthorised access to data for almost 27 million subscribers. That's over half the population of South Korea (50M). Over HALF the population!

According to the regulator's report, SK Telecom failed at every layer of defence and effectively rolled-out the red carpet to their own systems for the cyber criminals that ran amok through their network.

Administrators had left thousands of server credentials in plaintext on a management server; there was no password protection on subscriber databases; IDS logs were not reviewed by teams; 26 Million USIM authentication keys were left unencrypted in the database (leading to the ability to clone SIMS without knowledge); And no response from monitoring teams on the installation of malware and exfiltration of data.

The regulator provided a scathing observation "The security operating environment between the internet and the internal network was managed and operated in a state that was very vulnerable to illegal intrusion". Read that as corporate speak for "step away from the computer, Ralf Wiggum, it's time to go eat crayons"

Not only slapped with a hefty fine, SK Telecom have now been ordered to implement a range of remedial items including proper encryption, tighter access controls and real-time monitoring of IDS. Just a few things that any Telco should have been implementing by default, you would have imagined.

Telecommunication companies are a high value target that we often only see nation-backed or heavyweight groups going after, but this incident proves the bar for security is not guaranteed to start at certain levels. When you leave your networks wide open with all the goodies on the table, don't be surprised if someone walks off with the food, pooch, car keys, bank cards and chaise longue.

That's enough Internets for me today.

Microsoft Cloud Takeover Ransomware

Ransomware has been a continual thorn in the side of business for several years now and much of the reported incidents (not everyone does still it seems) are related to systems on corporate network fleets. In a continually "cloud-first" narrative for business, it appears that cybercriminal groups are also chanting the "cloud-first" mantra and evolving their attack patterns.

Microsoft's threat intelligence team have caught one of the financially-motivated groups they monitor, Storm-0501, breaking into an enterprise's on-premesis and cloud environments, exfiltrating and destroying resident data before demanding a ransom to get the data back. The attack does not rely on normal malware deployment and saw the enterprise contacted over their own Teams platform to demand the payment.

The attacks seem to pivot from on-premesis systems to the cloud environment through lateral movement and privilege escalation, with the use of Evil-WinRM for remote code execution. DCSync attacked Active Directory and stolen credentials were then used to target cloud-connected systems and ultimately register an Entra ID tenant owned by the criminal group as a trusted federated domain, creating a backdoor to the systems.

The multi-stage processes show a determined and resourceful group with understanding in on-premesis and cloud environments with a new angle on the ransomware process.

Microsoft have made a change to Entra ID that aims to prevent attackers from abusing the Directory Synchronisation Accounts in attacks to escalate privileges. They also advise using the Trusted Platform Module on Entra Connect Sync Server to store sensitive credentials and cryptographic keys, preventing those from being stolen by attackers.

The principles of least-privilege and zero trust should be considered with these hybrid environments, and as always - MFA.

Kill Switch Dev gets 4 years

The end of employment can be a traumatic experience and humans can respond in many ways. The response to their termination displays everything about their character, and for Davis Lu, a 55-year old Chinese national living legally in Houston, it spoke volumes about the vindictive nature of his personality.

Davis worked for an Ohio-based company from 2007 until 2019 and after a corporate restructuring and subsequent demotion in 2018, he began to embed malicious code throughout the corporate Windows environment. His malware included infinite Java thread loops designed to overwhelm servers and crash production systems along with a kill switch named "IsDLEnabledinAD" that would automatically lock all users out of their accounts if Davis's account was disabled in Active Directory.

When Davis's employment was terminated on the 9th September 2019, the kill switch activated and caused thousands of users to be locked out of their systems. The disruption in services caused the company to loose hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Davis was instructed to return his laptop as part of the termination of employment and reportedly deleted encrypted data from his device. Investigators later discovered search queries on the device researching how to elevate privileges, hide processes and quickly delete files.

Having been found guilty of intentionally causing damage to protected computers earlier in the year the sentencing has now landed Davis with a four-year prison sentence and then a three year supervised release following his prison term.

The case again highlights the importance of least-privilege access to systems and auditing. Humans will always react emotionally to events, but the business can often protect itself and other employees with simple controls and processes. Undoubtedly, Davis's case will not be the last we see hitting the news in the current uncertain climate of large business layoffs.

Cisco FMC Vulnerability

Another day, another Cisco vulnerability, and this one has a critical 10.0 CVSS rating. If this were a gymnastics competition, they'd be up there on the podium this year.

The CVE-2025-20265 is caused by an improper handling of user input by Firewall Management Center's RADIUS authentication subsystem during the login process. Thankfully, the exploit is only possible if RADIUS authentication is configured for the web-based management interface, SSH mangement or both.

Found during internal security testing by Cisco software engineer Brandon Sakai, the issue is not known to be exploited in the real world (yet!).

Cisco FMC is a central management platform for products such as their firewalls, IDS, URL filtering and anti-malware tools. Attacking this central hub to other devices on the corporate network is a prime target and likely to be actively prodded by foreign government groups.

Cisco warned in their security bulletin "An attacker could exploit this vulnerability by sending crafted input when entering credentials that will be authenticated at the configured RADIUS server, a successful exploit could allow the attacker to execute commands at a high privilege level."

More information on the issue is available on Cisco's Security Advisory page.

HTTP/2 Vulnerability to Crash

The HTTP/2 protocol, introduced in 2012 to provide a range of improvements on the original has become the most widely used web protocol, despite the newer HTTP/3 variant being released in 2019. The introduction of HTTP/2 brought a huge range of improvements on the original, however it also (as with all new software) brought a range of new bugs into the world.

In 2023, the 'Rapid Reset' flaw brought the protocol into the limelight with the ability to DoS servers. Having undergone a patch to correct this flaw, it now appears as if the patch wasn't as complete as one might hope. Security researchers Gal Bar Nahum, Anat Bremler-Barr, and Yaniv Harel have released details of a "common design flaw" in implementations of HTTP/2 allowing the possibility of "massive DoS attacks"

CVE-2025-8671 which extends the previous CVE-2023-44487 "Rapid Reset" vulnerability allows an attacker to bypass concurrency limits which would normally prevent a server from accepting requests for too much work. Rapid Reset allowed the bypass of limits by quickly cancelling a request, however in MadeYouReset they can trick the server into cancelling the request on the client's behalf which bypasses the protection put in place in 2023.

The vulnerability discovery partially supported by Thales' Imperva has further detail and a range of mitigation strategies on their blog.

Even "well-crafted" requests and traffic can be used in weaponised form, it would continue to appear.

Satellite Hacking

Researchers from VisionSpace Technologies displayed a range of vulnerabilities in satellite operations software at BlackHat. The team, having undertaken responsible disclosure beforehand, demoed several major flaws in software used at ground stations and onbobard craft, displaying the posibility of takeover or denial of service.

Space is increasingly becoming a crowded and sensitive operational zone, with the cost of delivering equipment to orbit reducing each year. Communication with orbital craft occurs over RF, with newer equipment undertaking key exchange for encrypted communication. It is possible it seems, however, to crash orbital platforms with an unencrypted message, and then force a rotation of keys.

"So basically, you send a packet to the spacecraft, and the entire software crashes and reboots, which then actually causes the spacecraft, if it's not properly configured, to reset all its keys. And then you have zero keys on the spacecraft that you can use from that stage on.", Milenko Starcik announced.

The issues discovered were not solely related to craft, but also the software used for tasking such as Yamcs and OpenC3Cosmos which allowed a range of cross-site-scripting and take control over satellite equipment.

The team's discovery has provided a range of security improvement to the operation of craft able to be updated, however it is likely that out of the thousands of orbital craft in the sky, many of them will be vulnerable to a range of attack vectors. Space may or may not be the final frontier of cybersecurity, it seems.

KLM and Air France Targeted

KLM and Air France have become the latest in a series of air travel companies to be targeted by cybercriminal groups attempting to access and exfiltrate critical data.

The airlines who are part of the same group detected unusual activity on an external platform utilised by KLM and Air France customer service. This appears to have led to unauthorised access to customer data. The security teams acted quickly to stop the unauthorised access, however the firms have not publicly specified the types of data stolen, other than citing "No sensitive data, such as passwords, travel details, Flying Blue miles, passport or credit card information was stolen".

It would appear that firstname, surname and contact details have been disclosed in the breach from shared images of documents sent to customers.

The airlines have advised customers to be on the alert for phishing attempts using the limited details acquired from the customer system.

It's not known as yet who the perpetrator of the incident was, however it has all the hallmarks of the ShinyHunters group.

Google Salesforce Breach

Google has confirmed this week cybercriminals broke into one of it's Salesforce databases in June and pulled data belonging to some of it's SMB customers.

The Threat intelligence team has admitted a gang associated with ShinyHunters broke into one of the corporate Salesforce instances and accessed contact information and related notes.

Google advised "The data retrieved by the threat actor was confined to basic and largely publicly-available business information, such as business names and contact details"

The incident continues to highlight that even the most forward-security-thinking companies can suffer breaches to compontents in it's estate.
