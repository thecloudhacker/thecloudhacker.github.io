Romanian Land Registry Hack

On Tuesday 14th July, the Romanian cadastre application, known as e-Terra, and internal email systems went offline. This very quickly led the Romania's real-estate market grinding to a halt as notaries could not authenticate sales, register mortgages, or it's citizens pull proof of ownership.

Romania's National Cyber Security Directorate (DNSC) has confirmed the attack was not a complex campaign and would have been prevented with some simple steps:
Known, unpatched vulnerabilities (that, according to the DNSC, had recently flagged to the Land Registry), combined with leaked credentials that were posted online, and a Group Policy named "DISABLE WINDOWS FIREWALL" led to the compromise. There was no zero-day, and the attacker did not need any form of bypass.

By Wednesday, stolen data was being offered for sale online in hacking forums and information surrounding the environment at the Land Registry was being offered-up.

ThreatLocker has a full write-up of the incident, highlighting how even simple, traditional methods can lead to disasterous implications without the need for APT-level technology to bring national services to a halt.

If ever there was a call to review your estate, policy configuration and vulnerability management programme, this is it.

Canada Secretly Cleans Botnet-Infected Devices

The Canadian Security Intelligence Service was given a warrant for threat reduction measures in a first-of-it's-kind ruling allowing them to "alter, degrade, and destroy botnet data on the infected machines and cut the devices loose from the networks". In other words, hack into devices, remove the crud and patch the infrastructure without the owner's intervention.

Involved in the Botnet were Canada-based servers, small office and home office routers, Internet-of-things devices, Ring doorbells, Security Cameras, TV's and Wifi-enabled devices.

Whilst the motive of preventing loss, theft of details, denial of service over services and further illegal activity, the modification of services and hardware with persistent fixes presents an ethical conundrum. Canada's court found the threat clearly established and imminent with the measures deemed necessary, reasonable and proportional. Do the end users and customers, however?

Reaching into a device that is not your own and wiping data is classified as computer mischief under the Criminal Code, hence the required court ruling before undertaking the work. It is not clear, however, how the agency collected information leading up to the request.

Should the customers have been notified first or in the process? Answers on a postcard...

Axios Supply Chain Hack

The HTTP client, Axios, has been the victim of a supply chain attack after two new published versions (1.14.1 and 0.30.4) of the npm package included a malicious dependency, injecting plain-crypto-js.

The two versions were found to have been published using compromised npm credentials of the primary Axios maintainer, allowing the attackers to bypass the project's GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline. Socket have a nice breakdown of the issue with details of the payload.

Users who have Axios versions 1.14.1 or 0.30.4 installed are required to immediately downgrade to a safe version and rotate credentials. The malicious versions have now been removed from npm.

Anyone who has installed the affected versions is also advised to remove plain-crypto-js form the node_modules directory and check for RAT artifacts. It is also advised to audit your CI/CD pipelines and block egress traffic to the command-and-control domain "sfrclak dot com".

Supply chain compromises will continue to be an ongoing issue as they work and fly under the radar in many cases. As always, lock your deployed software versions, ensure you have pipeline security scanning and maintain cool-off periods before deploying new versions anywhere near systems, never bleeding-edge production!

LiteLLM Compromised

Python Package Index have removed two versions of LiteLLM, the open source interface for accessing multiple large language models after a supply-chain attack injected them with credential-stealing code.

LiteLLM v1.82.7 and v1.82.8 have been removed due to malware in the litellm_init.pth file. The compromise appears to have originated form the use of Trivy in the project's CI/CD pipeline. According to details from Aqua Security, the attackers took advantage of a misconfiguration in Triby's GitHub Actions environment to steal a privileged access token allowing the manipulation of CI/CD, and the subsequent push of malicious Trivy versions ( v0.69.5 and v0.69.6 ) as DockerHub images.

The advisory warns anyone who has installed and run the project should assume any credentials available to the LiteLLM environment have been exposed and should be revoked or rotated immediately.

FCC Blocks Foreigners

In a country where Cisco kit used to be tampered with before export, the Government have now decided they don't want to potentially import foreign back doors into their marketplace through hardware and software.

The FCC have created a list of vendors that will now be unable to send new equipment to the US until they are verified and approved .

The manufacturer equipment is "deemed to pose an unacceptable risk to the national security of the United States or the security and safety of United States persons, based exclusively on any of four sources for such a determination and that such equipment or services possess certain capabilities as enumerated in section 2(a) of the Secure and Trusted Communications Networks Act of 2019", according to the ruling.

What it means in practice is that vendors are now selling the stock they have and citizens are free to utilise equipment they own, but new imports are blocked until they are cleared by the FCC.

The ruling determines "Any new router made outside the US will now need to be approved by the FCC before it can be imported, marketed, or sold in the country.", which is ironic as Cisco and Netgear build their equipment off-shore. Starlink is one of the only manufacturers to actually build equipment out of the 'States.

What will this mean in practice for organisations? Either higher costs for equipment, or utilisation of older, out-of-date equipment leading to potentially weaker security, most likely. Expect eBay prices for routers to go through the roof over the coming year.

Telnet Flaw enables unauthenticated root RCE

Dream Security have disclosed a flaw in Telnet that is tracked as CVE-2026-32746 and carries a CVSS score of 9.8. Discovered last week, it affects all versions of the Telnet service implementation through 2.7 and, through sending a specially crafted message during the initial connection handshake, can result in remote code execution as root.

Those out there sneering at the use of Telnet in today's security domain probably haven't worked with the layers of legacy equipment that still relies on the protocol for remote connection.

"No login is required; the bug is hit during option negotiation, before the login prompt. The overflow corrupts memory and can be turned into arbitrary writes. In practice, this can lead to remote code execution. Because telnetd usually runs as root (e.g., under inetd or xinetd), a successful exploit would give the attacker full control of the system" Adiel Sol from Dream Security writes.

In the absence of a current fix, users are advised to:

  • Disable the telnetd service if it is not required
  • Run telnetd without root privileges where it is required
  • Block port 23 at the network perimeter and host-based firewall level to restrict access and isolate Telnet access

AWS CodeBuild Flaw

The security company, Wiz, discovered a critical flaw in AWS CodeBuild ( dubbed CodeBreach by the team ) that could have potentially led to the complete takeover of AWS's own GitHub repositories and put every AWS account in the world at risk. The team noted it to be potentially worse than the SolarWinds supply chain attack.

Discovered back in August 2025, the team contacted AWS who then fixed the security hole in September 2025 before the system could be compromised by groups or agencies.

The team details the severity of the issue sternly in their finding report: "The issue allowed a complete takeover of key AWS GitHub repositories - most notably the AWS JavaScript SDK, a core library that powers the AWS Console. By exploiting CodeBreach, attackers could have injected malicious code to launch a platform-wide compromise, potentially affecting not just the countless applications depending on the SDK, but the Console itself, threatening every AWS account."

In a tale as old as time, a couple of characters in a Regex filter allowed unauthenticated attackers to infiltrate the build environment and leak privileged credentials. Once again, the CI/CD pipeline is in focus as a security pain point. Whilst no action is necessary in this instance to resolve the primary issue, the team are advising a range of security measures to prevent untrusted pull requests triggering privileged builds and securing your GitHub connection with unique fine-grained personal access tokens for each CodeBuild project.

A HUGE thanks need to go to the Wiz team by the world's user populous and a stern question for all our AWS account managers of "why the hell am I hearing about this in global security news months after the event and not from you directly?".

New n8n CVE

A new year, a new vulnerability in software the world is using merrily. The latest vulnerability in n8n comes hot on the heels of the previous CVE in December which also allowed attackers to execute commands.

The new vulnerability ( CVE-2025-68668 ) is classified as a Protection Mechanism Failure, rated at 9.9 on the "oh crud!" meter. Affecting versions 1.0.0 through to 2.0.0, any authenticated user with permission to create or modify workflows to execute arbitrary operating system commands on the host running n8n

n8n have confirmed they implemented a "task runner-based native Python implementation in version 1.111.0 as an optional feaature for improved security isolation". The feature can be enabled using the N8N_RUNNERS_ENABLED and N8N_NATIVE_PYTHON_RUNNER environment variables prior to 2.0.0 where it is enabled by default.

As workarounds, n8n is recommending that users:

  1. Disable the Code Node by setting the environment variable NODES_EXCLUDE: "[\"n8n-nodes-base.code\"]"
  2. Disable Python support in the Code node by setting the environment variable N8N_PYTHON_ENABLED=false
  3. Configure n8n to use the task runner-based Python sandbox via the N8N_RUNNERS_ENABLED and N8N_NATIVE_PYTHON_RUNNER environment variables

As adoption of powerful and easy-to-use tooling becomes more normal, it's likely to find itself a target for higher scrutiny by nefarious parties.

MongoDB Bleeds Memory Data

A flaw in Zlib compression protocol header length ( CVE-2025-14847 ) which is utilised by default in MongoDB can return uninitialized heap memory without authenticating to the server.

Tracked by MongoDB and thankfully with a patch available ( MongoDB Jira Ticket), the MongoDB team urge users to patch immediately even though there are no current reports of active exploitation.

This data leak vulnerability affects the following versions:

  • MongoDB 8.2.0 through 8.2.3
  • MongoDB 8.0.0 through 8.0.16
  • MongoDB 7.0.0 through 7.0.26
  • MongoDB 6.0.0 through 6.0.26
  • MongoDB 5.0.0 through 5.0.31
  • MongoDB 4.4.0 through 4.4.29
  • All MongoDB Server v4.2 versions
  • All MongoDB Server v4.0 versions
  • All MongoDB Server v3.6 versions

The team have also advised "If you cannot upgrade immediately, disable zlib compression on the MongoDB Server by starting mongod or mongos with a networkMessageCompressors or a net.compression.compressors option that explicitly omits zlib. Example safe values include snappy,zstd or disabled"

Cisco AsyncOS Zero Day

'Twas the week before Christmas and all through the NOC, Cisco Admins were screaming and Criminals running amok.

Cisco have announced a critical vulnerability in the AsyncOS software under active exploitation by Chinese APT's. The attack campaign affects Cisco Secure Email Gateway systems (physical AND virtual) and Cisco Secure Web Manager appliances (physical AND virtual) of ALL release versions when these conditions are met:

  • The appliance is configured with the Spam Quarantine feature
  • The Spam Quarantine feature is exposed to and reachable from the Internet

Cisco have uncovered exploitation dating back to at least late November 2025 with the vulnerability being used to drop tunneling tools like ReverseSSH and Chisel. A lightweight Python backdoor has been also seen capable of receiving encoded commands and executing them. The CVE ( CVE-2025-20393 ) has a rating score of 10.0 and in the absence of a patch, administrators are being urged to restore their appliances to a secure configuration; limit access from the Internet; secure devices behind a firewall and only allow traffic from trusted hosts; separate mail and management functionality onto a separate network interface; monitor web log traffic for unexpected traffic and disable HTTP for the main administration portal.
